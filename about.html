// origins

I never planned to be an engineer. For six years, I led my high school's Model UN team, where I spent a lot of time working on technology-related proposals for the UN General Assembly. The problems were often clear, but I kept running into the same frustration: I could explain what needed to happen, but I didn’t yet have the tools to build it myself. That gap is what pulled me toward computer engineering.

// university

Once I got there, I realized I was interested in more than just building systems. I wanted to understand what those systems were actually doing, especially when they were meant to model human intelligence. That led me to double minor in psychology and cognitive science, where I started thinking more seriously about how intelligence works in humans and how it gets represented in machines.

// career

This curiosity carried over to my work at the University of Waterloo's TRuST Network, where I studied how inductive reasoning and overgeneralization affect model performance on statistical minority groups. I was interested in how those failure modes could be made visible in ways that were useful beyond the technical side. Outside of academia, the internships I pursued shifted my focus to deployment. Most notably, at RBC Borealis, I worked on a project that exposes a high-profile financial dataset to 17k employees through an agentic interface, where I was able to observe how machine intelligence often falls short of replicating or augmenting human intelligence in a real-world setting, and how this minute gap can have significant consequences. This begs the question: how can we advance machine intelligence in a way that is both technically advanced and human-centric?

// extracurriculars

Being part of the IPC's advisory body made made the policy side harder to ignore. In working on the IPC's AI and privacy policy, including drafting the Charter on Digital Security in secondary schoolsI saw how governance moves at a pace slower than the systems being regulated. That gap made it hard to separate my technical work from its broader effects in practice.
